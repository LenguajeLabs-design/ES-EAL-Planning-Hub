Learning Pathways

Phase support and language progression

SSIS Phase 1

Students needing targeted literacy and oral language support

WIDA literacy score: 3.0 and below

  • Strong emphasis on speaking, listening, foundational reading, and sentence formation.
  • Heavy use of visuals, modeling, sentence frames, and guided practice.
  • In Grade 5, Chinese may be replaced with EAL English for Phase 1 students.

SSIS Phase 2

Students accessing grade-level content with focused intervention

WIDA literacy score: 3.0 to 4.0

  • In-class support plus targeted reading and writing intervention groups.
  • Small-group work based on MAP, ORF, MAZE, phonics, and classroom data.
  • Support shifts toward text analysis, writing structure, and academic language.

WIDA Entering

Likely entry point for many new or early learners

Students at the beginning stages of English development

  • Language purposes: describing, explaining, identifying, informing
  • Grammar: nouns, verbs, adjectives, prepositions, pronouns, articles, numerals
  • Listening: supporting details, sequencing, comparing, summarizing, inferencing

WIDA Emerging

Where students start to expand into academic language

Students moving toward more independent academic language use

  • Language purposes: describing, explaining, informing, comparing, evaluating, analyzing, justifying
  • Grammar: conjunctions, adverbs, modals, irregular forms, comparatives, gerunds
  • Listening: main idea, summarizing, inferencing, comparing and contrasting

Year Map

A practical Grade 4-5 scope and sequence

This sample sequence is designed for mixed Grade 4 and 5 EAL groups where most students are in Entering and Emerging bands.

Quarter 1

Build survival English and core sentence patterns

Focus on identifying, describing, and informing.

  • WIDA focus: Entering, with some learners beginning to move into Emerging
  • Grammar: nouns, pronouns, articles, numerals, adjectives, prepositions, simple present, present continuous
  • Themes: classroom community, routines, family, basic descriptive language

Quarter 2

Move into explanation and comparison

Students begin stretching beyond naming into short explanations.

  • WIDA focus: Entering to Emerging
  • Grammar: possessives, demonstratives, irregular nouns, object pronouns, simple future, conjunctions
  • Themes: communities, weather, food, habitats, daily life

Quarter 3

Expand academic language

Students work more consistently with explanation, comparison, and early justification.

  • WIDA focus: Emerging
  • Grammar: auxiliary verbs, modals, compound prepositions, irregular verbs, comparatives, adverbs of frequency
  • Themes: science, geography, biographies, problem solving

Quarter 4

Strengthen independence and justification

Students consolidate emerging proficiency and prepare for more independent work.

  • WIDA focus: strong Emerging, with some students moving toward Developing
  • Grammar: subordinating conjunctions, modal review, comparative structures, superlative forms
  • Themes: environment, inventions, world cultures, project work

Teaching Moves

What instruction should look like

Balanced literacy every day

The SSIS framework names a predictable workshop model: language activator, mini-lesson or guided practice, independent work, and share or closing.

Scaffold before independence

Visuals, sentence frames, read-alouds, guided practice, partner talk, and multimodal input are central for Entering and Emerging learners.

Teach language through content

WIDA targets become more powerful when embedded inside Readers and Writers units and Units of Inquiry instead of being taught in isolation.

Suggested weekly rhythm

  1. Day 1: vocabulary, visual input, language target
  2. Day 2: guided reading or shared text, grammar in context
  3. Day 3: listening and speaking task with small-group support
  4. Day 4: sentence or paragraph writing with frames
  5. Day 5: review, oral check, short performance task, or targeted support and catch-up

Phase 1 planning priorities

  • Build oral language through structured talk, role play, and repetition.
  • Teach foundational reading through phonemic awareness, decoding, and supported comprehension.
  • Use guided writing, sentence frames, and strong models for sentence construction.

Phase 2 intervention priorities

  • Use small groups to target structure, sentence expansion, and organization.
  • Teach comprehension strategies like predicting, questioning, summarizing, and inferring.
  • Keep vocabulary instruction tied to current texts and inquiry themes.

WIDA Can Do Descriptors

Where this new document fits best

The Grade 4-5 Can Do Descriptors are a strong bridge between WIDA expectations and daily classroom planning. They help teachers see what learners at Entering, Emerging, and Developing levels can realistically do across listening, speaking, reading, and writing.

  • Use them when planning differentiated tasks for the same unit.
  • Use them to set realistic language goals for students in each WIDA band.
  • Use them to check whether a task is too hard, too easy, or just right.

Key Uses

Best planning match for this site

This document organizes language around four key uses that fit very naturally into unit planning and performance tasks.

  • Recount: retell, report, summarize experiences or information
  • Explain: show how or why something happens
  • Argue: give an opinion or claim with reasons or evidence
  • Discuss: participate in talk to build meaning with others

Assessment

Use data to group, support, and adjust

Assessment sources named in the SSIS framework

  • Mini-WIDA and WIDA MODEL
  • MAP and MAP Reading Fluency
  • MAZE and oral reading fluency
  • CORE phonics, PAST, UFLI checks, encoding
  • On-demand writing, published writing, anecdotal records, running records

Simple classroom assessment flow

  • Weekly: oral language check, listening task, vocabulary check, sentence sample
  • Unit end: short speaking, writing, or compare-and-explain performance task
  • Termly: revisit WIDA-linked goals and re-group students based on current evidence

Simple way to use the Can Do Descriptors in planning

Keep your unit topic the same, then adjust the language demand by WIDA level. For example, in an animals and habitats unit:

  • Entering: name animals, match habitat words, say short phrases with visual support
  • Emerging: compare two habitats, retell a short text, state simple reasons
  • Developing: explain cause and effect, discuss evidence, justify an opinion
